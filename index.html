Hidden Suffragette HQ to have plaque put up 100 years after women given the vote

The Suffragettes of Sheffield will be commemorated with a plaque outside a hidden high-street shop next month, after a temporary one was unveiled this week.

A crowd of around 50 people were outside the shop on Chapel Walk on Tuesday as Lord Mayor Anne Murphy gave a speech on the centenary of women’s suffrage.

The Labour councillor put up the provisional plaque and spoke about the importance of those she called ‘our sisters’ in helping to gain equality.

Chrissy Meleady MBE, who is the chief executive of the Equality and Human Rights Commission UK, said: “The plaque is absolutely vital because of Sheffield’s position in these movements.

“This was the first place where the suffragettes came together and it hasn’t been given much recognition.”

The opening on Tuesday was the 100th anniversary of the Representation of the People Act 1918- where women gained the right to vote for the first time.

Nikki Bond, Sheffield Central Labour Party’s Women’s Officer, set up a crowdfunding page to raise money for the plaque.

"It's about being invested in Sheffield."

She said that only last Monday the lord mayor told her they were struggling to pay for the plaque.

She decided to raise the money herself and reached £700 in five days, after two donations of over £100.

She said the plaque will give people a sense of ownership over a part of Sheffield’s history that they can then come and see.

She said: “It’s about people being invested in Sheffield’s history and in something which is so important for women in Sheffield as well.

“We have such a proud history of strong Sheffield women, including the statue to the women of steel.”

She added: “The history shows itself in Sheffield’s leading women today. The lord mayor is a woman, so is the leader of the council, and the deputy leader is a woman.”

A permanent plaque is expected to be ready for international women’s day on 5 March and a local democracy day for women is being organised to follow.

Children doing research in 2010 found the unoccupied shop, most recently used by the sweet company CandyCo, was the Sheffield headquarters for the Suffragettes in 1908.

The shop was a centre for the Women’s Social and Political Union and sold memorabilia to support the cause.

It doubled as the WSPU office and it was one of the first in the country.

Ms Meleady said: “Shop owners mixed with the suffragettes and the cooperative women’s guild- who also held meetings there- and fought for universal emancipation.”

"The plaque is absolutely vital."

The WSPU was led by Adela Pankhurst after she was sent by her mother Emmeline to organise the movement in Sheffield.

Emmeline formed the group in 1903 to campaign for women’s right to vote and she played a central role.

Adela lived at 45 Marlborough Road, Broomhill, and applied a more militant form of protest.

She led a demonstration of over 800 unemployed women through the city centre in 1908 and secretly tried to gain access to the Cutler’s Feast- by disguising herself as a kitchen maid.

She was discovered so gave a speech outside the Town Hall about the injustices facing women in society.

Jill Liddington, an expert in women’s history at the University of Leeds and author of One Hand Tied Behind Us, said: “I think it would be an excellent idea to have a plaque.”

Millicent Fawcett, founder of the NUWSS, one of the largest suffrage groups, was commemorated with a plaque in Cambridge on Tuesday.
